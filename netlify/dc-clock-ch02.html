Heat Rises

The roof of Clonshaugh Data Centre was not designed to be walked. It was designed to hold things. Condensing units in rows, exhaust fans, pipework lagged in grey insulation that had started to peel at the elbows. Cable trays leading nowhere in particular. A decade's worth of decisions about where to put the next piece of kit, none of them made with the next person in mind.

Mark walked it like he'd walked a hundred roofs. Slowly. Not because he needed to think — he was already thinking — but because hurrying a roof like this meant missing things, and missing things on a roof meant finding them later, at the worst possible time.

He had a thermal imaging camera on a strap around his neck and a clipboard under his arm. He hadn't used either yet. He was counting.

clockQuote 1: "Before you look at what the instruments say, count what's there. The number tells you what the design assumed. The instruments tell you what's actually happening."

He counted nine condensing units. Four on the north side, five on the south. Each one a squat metal box about the size of a chest freezer, fans on top, refrigerant pipework running down through roof penetrations into the building below. They were R-410A units — he'd confirmed that from the data plates before he came up, crouching at each one for thirty seconds, writing in his clipboard with the unhurried efficiency of someone who'd done this particular task before and knew that legibility mattered more than speed.

R-410A. GWP of 2,088. The EU had been clear about the trajectory of that number since the F-Gas regulation came into force — the current version, EU 2024/573, was not the last word on the subject, but it was clear enough. Refrigerant with a GWP above 750 was already in the phase-down. These nine units were, in regulatory terms, a clock counting down.

That wasn't today's problem. Today's problem was the number nine.

He stood at the centre of the roof and looked at the arrangement again. Four and five. He wrote 9 condensing units. N+1 would require 10 for 9 active circuits. Confirm which is duty, which is standby. Then he put a question mark next to N+1, because a question mark was the right thing to put when you were standing on a roof in Dublin and you weren't sure if the person who built this had thought about what happened when the one in the corner stopped working in July.

Declan was waiting at the roof access door when Mark came back down the internal staircase.

"Well?" Declan said.

"Nine circuits," Mark said.

"That's what it says on the asset register."

"Does the asset register say which one's the spare?"

Declan looked at him for a moment. "They all run together."

Mark wrote something in his clipboard. "Right," he said.

It wasn't the sound of a man who'd heard something reassuring.

Downstairs, Ann was standing at the end of Hall A with a coffee she'd stopped tasting twenty minutes ago. She was watching two things: the data hall and Mark, in that order. She'd been watching data halls for three years — as the fund's representative, she'd visited twelve of them, on three continents, in various states of health and distress. She had learned to read rooms. This room, right now, was telling her something she couldn't quite name.

The hot aisle was at the back of every row. Cold at the front, hot at the back, cold at the front, hot at the back — alternating down the length of the hall in a pattern that was so fundamental to data centre design it had its own name, its own acronym, its own body of practice. Hot-aisle cold-aisle containment. She'd learned that in her first site visit and she'd never forgotten it, because the person who'd explained it had done so in a single sentence: every watt of electricity going in becomes a watt of heat coming out, and your job is to make sure the heat goes where you want it.

Declan had said that to her yesterday. She'd written it in her notebook.

clockQuote 2: "A data centre isn't a computing facility. It's a heat management facility that also runs computers. When you understand that, you understand everything."

The problem — and she was starting to understand that there was a problem, even if she couldn't yet say what it was — was visible in the hot aisle at the back of Row 7. She could see it from here, which meant it wasn't subtle. The containment curtain at the top of the aisle was sagging on one side, a gap of perhaps twenty centimetres where the Velcro had separated from the rail. Through that gap, hot air — the exhaust from forty-eight servers — was leaking out into the cold aisle on the other side.

It was twenty centimetres of warm air. It was also twenty centimetres of the cooling system working harder than it needed to, paying for heat it had already moved once.

clockQuote 3: "Hot air and cold air in the wrong place is not an airflow problem. It's a money problem. It's just that the bill arrives at the end of the year instead of the end of the day."

Mark appeared at her shoulder without her hearing him arrive. She'd noticed that about him already — he moved quietly for a large man.

"What are you looking at?" he said.

She pointed at the curtain in Row 7.

He looked at it. "How long has that been like that?"

"I don't know," she said. "I've only been here since Monday."

He walked to the row, looked up at the curtain, looked down at the floor. He crouched and pressed his hand flat to the raised floor tile.

"Cold air delivery's through the floor," he said, not quite to her — more to himself, or to the building. "Raised floor plenum, perforated tiles under each row. The CRAH units pressurise the plenum and the cold air comes up." He stood up. "If you've got bypass — hot air getting into the cold aisle, cold air getting to where there's no load — you're paying for cooling that isn't doing any work."

"How much bypass does this facility have?"

He looked at the ceiling, then at the floor, then at the row of racks nearest them. "I'd estimate thirty-five percent," he said. "That's a guess until I can instrument it properly. But you can usually tell."

Thirty-five percent. She did the arithmetic quietly. Clonshaugh: 2.4 megawatts IT load. Total facility load at PUE 1.50 was 3.6 megawatts. That meant 1.2 megawatts going to everything that wasn't the servers — cooling, lighting, power conversion losses, the lot. A third of the cooling effort going sideways. She was not an engineer. But she understood percentages.

"Is that normal?" she asked.

"It's common," Mark said. "Common and normal aren't the same thing."

clockQuote 4: "Common means you'll see it everywhere. Normal means it's acceptable. A lot of things in data centres are common that shouldn't be normal."

The CRAH units were along the north wall of Hall A — six of them, grey metal cabinets two metres tall, each one about the width of a wardrobe. CRAH: Computer Room Air Handler. Unlike the condensing units on the roof, which ran on refrigerant and moved heat directly, these moved chilled water — cold water in from the chiller plant, through a coil, fans pushing air across it, air into the raised floor plenum at eight or nine degrees Celsius. The return water came back warmer. The difference between supply and return temperature — the delta-T — told you how hard each unit was working.

Mark opened the service panel on Unit 3 and looked at the flow meter. He took out his phone and photographed the reading, then photographed the supply temperature gauge and the return temperature gauge.

He stood with the numbers for a moment.

"What does it say?" Declan asked. He'd been trailing them around the floor, hands in his pockets, watching Mark the way a homeowner watches a plumber who's making a face at the pipework under the sink.

"Supply is eight point four degrees. Return is eleven point one."

"So?"

"Design delta-T for chilled water in a facility like this is typically ten to twelve degrees. You want a big spread — cold water comes in, hot water goes back, efficient heat transfer. What you're getting here is two point seven degrees."

Declan said nothing.

clockQuote 5: "A low delta-T is the cooling system's way of telling you it's working twice as hard to do half the job. The water's not picking up enough heat. Either the coils are fouled, the airflow is wrong, or the water's moving faster than it needs to. In any case, something upstream is not right."

"Two point seven," Ann said quietly.

"Two point seven," Mark confirmed. "It means the chilled water is arriving, passing through the unit, and leaving almost as cold as it came in. It's not transferring heat effectively. Could be coil fouling. Could be the balancing valves are out. Could be the chiller's working on low load and the delta-T drifts down as a result." He closed the panel. "I'd want to see all six units before I say which."

He went to Unit 4. Same sequence: door, flow, temperatures, photograph, close.

Unit 4 was 2.9 degrees.

Unit 5 was 3.1 degrees.

He went to Unit 6, which was in the far corner near the electrical riser. He opened the panel. Looked at the gauges. Wrote the numbers down.

"This one's at 4.2," he said. "Better. Still not great, but better." He looked at the duct above the unit, then at the floor tiles below it, then at the rack row in front of it. "This row's lower density — I can see from the power strips. Less heat to shift. The unit's working lighter."

Declan said, "The cooling's always run fine. We've never had a thermal shutdown."

"That's not the same as running efficiently," Mark said. His tone was not unkind. It was the tone of a man stating a fact that did not require either emotion or apology — just acknowledgement.

clockQuote 6: "'We've never had a problem' describes the past. It says nothing about how close to the edge you've been, or what happens next week when the load changes. It's evidence of luck as much as it's evidence of good engineering."

Declan looked at the floor. He wasn't angry. He looked like a man who had been proud of something, and was in the early stages of understanding that pride had not been entirely warranted.

Back on the roof, after lunch, Mark photographed the condensing units with the thermal camera. The images came out in false colour — cold surfaces blue and green, warm surfaces amber and red. Eight of the nine units showed similar thermal profiles: heat rejection working normally, air in at roughly eighteen degrees ambient, exhaust air at thirty-two, thirty-four. Rejecting heat to the atmosphere as designed.

The ninth unit — second from the east end on the south side — showed a different pattern. A patch of blue in the middle of the compressor housing. Not cold in a way that made sense. Cold in a way that suggested the compressor wasn't working at full load, or wasn't working at all.

clockQuote 7: "Thermal imaging doesn't tell you what's wrong. It tells you where to look. The difference matters, because the camera sees symptoms and engineers diagnose causes."

He photographed it three times from different angles. He would come back with a pressure gauge tomorrow. But the nine condensing units had become, in his working hypothesis, eight condensing units plus one question mark, and the design had never assumed it needed a spare.

Ann found him by the roof access door at the end of the afternoon, writing in his clipboard with the same unhurried efficiency she'd noticed that morning. She'd been keeping a list of her own questions, which she now reviewed. She crossed out the ones she'd answered herself during the day and was left with three.

"The refrigerant," she said. "R-410A. Is that a problem?"

Mark considered the question in the way she was coming to recognise — not a pause of uncertainty, but a pause of precision, making sure the answer that came out was the right shape.

"Not immediately," he said. "EU 2024/573 is phasing down high-GWP refrigerants. R-410A has a GWP of 2,088, which puts it squarely in the phase-down schedule. You'll reach a point where the cost of refrigerant — if you can get it at all — starts to climb. And if one of these units needs a major recharge in the next three to five years, you may find yourself replacing the unit rather than the refrigerant, because the economics flip." He looked out across the roof. "It's not a fire, Ann. But it's a calendar."

clockQuote 8: "Regulatory phase-downs don't fail your system overnight. They make the repair economics worse every year until replacement becomes the only rational option. The question isn't if — it's when, and whether you planned for it."

She wrote: R-410A — GWP 2,088 — EU 2024/573 — phase-down — plan replacement horizon.

"And the delta-T problem," she said. "What fixes it?"

"Depends on the cause. Coil cleaning is cheap — a few thousand euro, maybe less. Balancing valves are more involved but not dramatic. If the chiller controls need retuning, that's a commissioning exercise." He turned a page in his clipboard. "The bigger conversation is whether the chilled water system as designed can handle the load if you add density. At 2.4 megawatts IT you're managing. At 3 megawatts you start to stress the margins. At 4 megawatts—" He stopped. "Has anyone talked to Ann about expansion plans?"

"I'm Ann," she said.

He looked at her. "I know. I'm asking whether your investment thesis assumes this facility stays at 400 racks."

She held his gaze for a moment. That was, she thought, exactly the right question, asked at exactly the right time.

"I need to call Sarah," she said.

clockQuote 9: "The cooling system was sized for the building as it was. If the building is going to become something different, the question isn't whether the cooling can cope — it's whether anyone has asked that question yet."

Declan caught Mark at the car park at half-five. The sun was low, catching the rooftop condensers at an angle that made them look briefly important.

"So," Declan said. "What's the verdict?"

Mark opened his car door. "Not a verdict today. Observations." He put his clipboard on the passenger seat. "The cooling runs. It runs inefficiently. The delta-T's low, you've got significant bypass airflow, and one condenser unit on the south side needs a closer look tomorrow." He looked back at the building. "Nothing that's going to cause a thermal shutdown tonight."

"But?"

Mark thought about whether to say the next part. He said it.

"If you add load — even ten percent — you'll find out how much margin you don't have." He got in the car. "I'll write it up properly. Tell them to start on the chiller."

Declan watched him drive out of the car park.

Nothing that's going to cause a thermal shutdown tonight.

He'd said that, and it should have been reassuring. Somehow it wasn't.

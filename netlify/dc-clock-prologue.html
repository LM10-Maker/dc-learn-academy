Prologue — The Building

The building didn't look like anything. Grey cladding, flat roof, a car park with room for thirty and spaces for twelve. It sat behind a low wall on an industrial estate north of the M50, between a tile warehouse and a logistics depot that never seemed to open. You could drive past it every morning for a year and never once wonder what it did.

Declan Murphy wondered every day. Not because he had to — because the building told him things, if he listened.

He badged through the lobby at six forty-five on a Tuesday in October, the way he had done most Tuesdays for the past ten years. The lobby was cool and quiet. Beyond the inner door, the hum began. Two point four megawatts of IT load ran behind that hum — four hundred racks across two halls, drawing power through transformers that had been energised in the same week he started. Hall A on the left, Hall B on the right, separated by a central corridor that smelled faintly of warm air and cable insulation. A smell he had stopped noticing years ago but would miss instantly if it changed.

He walked Hall A first. Rows of cabinets, front-to-back airflow, cold aisle containment panels sealed along the ceiling line. The overhead lighting was set to minimum — nobody else was here yet. The only light that mattered was the status LEDs. Green, green, green. A steady cascade of them running down each cabinet face like a city seen from altitude. He paused at Row 12, listened. The fans were even. He moved on.

Hall B was the same. It was always the same, and that was the point.

He took the corridor to the electrical intake room and unlocked it with the key he kept separate from his badge lanyard. The room was small, warm, thick with the scent of transformer oil. Two 10 kV ring main units. The MV switchgear that connected them to ESB Networks. Behind glass on the far wall — he'd framed it himself when the previous facilities manager left — the original connection agreement. Dated 2013. Maximum Import Capacity: 5 MVA. He glanced at it the way a driver glances at the fuel gauge on a car that has never broken down. Noted. Not questioned.

The building had been designed to a set of rules that existed in 2012 and built to a set of standards that were current in 2013. In the years since, other rules had been written — in Brussels, in Dublin, by regulators who had never visited an industrial estate north of the M50. Rules about efficiency. About carbon. About what a building like this would need to prove, and when. The distance between what Clonshaugh was and what it would need to become was growing. Not dramatically. Not yet visibly. But steadily, the way a clock moves when you're not watching it.

Declan locked the intake room, pocketed the key, and walked back through the corridor to the ops room. Everything was normal. He made a coffee, sat at his desk, and opened his laptop.

A calendar notification waited on screen. Nine o'clock. Site visit — fund manager. He didn't know her name yet.

He closed the notification and checked the BMS dashboard instead. Supply air temperatures, return air temperatures, chiller status, UPS load percentages. All where they should be. He drank his coffee slowly and listened to the building hum.

Outside, the M50 traffic was building. Inside, Clonshaugh Data Centre ran the way it always ran. Perfectly. For now.
